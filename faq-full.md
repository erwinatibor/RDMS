## 3. Oral Health — General Public

**Q: How do I keep my teeth healthy?**
A: Brush with fluoride toothpaste for two minutes twice a day, floss daily, reduce sugary food and drink, drink water, avoid tobacco, and visit a dentist every six months.

**Q: What causes tooth decay?**
A: Tooth decay is caused by bacteria that produce acids when they feed on sugars. These acids erode tooth enamel. Poor brushing and high sugar diets increase risk significantly.

**Q: What causes gum disease?**
A: Gum disease is caused by plaque buildup on teeth and gums. Risk factors include poor oral hygiene, smoking, diabetes, dry mouth, and certain medications.

**Q: What are the signs of gum disease?**
A: Red or swollen gums, bleeding when brushing, persistent bad breath, receding gums, tooth sensitivity, and loose teeth.

**Q: Is gum disease linked to heart disease?**
A: Yes. Bacteria from infected gums can enter the bloodstream and contribute to inflammation in the arteries, increasing the risk of heart disease and stroke.

**Q: Can gum disease affect my blood sugar?**
A: Yes. Gum disease can make blood sugar harder to control in people with diabetes. Treating gum disease has been shown to improve glucose management.

**Q: Is bad breath a dental problem?**
A: In most cases, yes. Persistent bad breath is usually caused by bacteria on the tongue, gum disease, dry mouth, or dental decay.

**Q: When should I see a dentist?**
A: If you have tooth pain, bleeding gums, swelling, a loose tooth, white or red patches in the mouth, difficulty chewing, or sensitivity that persists. Routine check-ups every six months are recommended.

**Q: What is a dental extraction?**
A: Removal of a tooth that is too damaged to save, infected, or causing problems. Done under local anesthesia — not painful during the procedure.

**Q: How often should I visit a dentist?**
A: A routine check-up every six months for adults and children. People with gum disease or high cavity risk may need more frequent visits.

**Q: What is fluoride and why does it matter?**
A: Fluoride is a mineral that strengthens tooth enamel and protects against decay. Found in toothpaste, some drinking water, and applied directly by dentists during cleanings.

**Q: Can oral health affect pregnancy?**
A: Yes. Gum disease during pregnancy is linked to premature birth and low birth weight. Pregnant women should maintain good oral hygiene and continue dental check-ups.

**Q: Is tooth sensitivity normal?**
A: Occasional sensitivity is common, but persistent sensitivity to hot, cold, or sweet foods can indicate worn enamel, exposed roots, or a cracked tooth.

**Q: What is halitosis?**
A: Halitosis is the medical term for chronic bad breath. It is usually caused by bacteria, gum disease, dry mouth, or systemic conditions. A dentist can identify the underlying cause.

## 4. Children and Oral Health

**Q: When should a child first see a dentist?**
A: By age one, or within six months of the first tooth appearing.

**Q: How do I clean my baby's teeth?**
A: Before teeth appear, clean gums with a soft damp cloth. Once teeth appear, use a soft baby toothbrush with a rice-sized amount of fluoride toothpaste.

**Q: At what age can children brush alone?**
A: Children should be supervised when brushing until at least age seven or eight.

**Q: What causes early childhood tooth decay?**
A: Putting babies to sleep with a bottle containing milk, juice, or sweetened liquids — known as baby bottle tooth decay.

**Q: Are baby teeth important?**
A: Yes. Baby teeth hold space for permanent teeth, support speech development, and are used for chewing. Decay in baby teeth can cause pain, infection, and problems with permanent teeth.

**Q: What are dental sealants?**
A: Thin coatings applied to the chewing surfaces of back teeth to protect against decay. Recommended for school-age children and highly effective as a preventive measure.

**Q: How does RDMS help with children's oral health?**
A: RDMS runs school-based dental hygiene education programs teaching correct brushing, diet and teeth, and when to see a dentist. Contact RDMS to bring this program to your school.
