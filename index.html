Who We Are

Uniting Rwanda's
Dental Community

Dento-Medical Society Rwanda (RDMS) is a registered non-profit organization founded on 1 July 2024, headquartered in Huye, Rwanda. We unite dental surgeons, therapists, and public health professionals to advance oral and systemic health nationwide.

In Rwanda, dentistry and oral health have long been overlooked within the healthcare system — leading to fragmented services, low public awareness, and inadequate preventive care that disproportionately affects vulnerable communities. RDMS exists to close that gap through collaborative education, research, outreach, and policy advocacy.

Oral Health Education

Your Mouth, Your Health

Oral health is connected to your heart, blood sugar, lungs, and immune system. The basics protect your whole body.

RDMS Key Fact

Research shows that untreated gum disease is linked to increased risk of heart disease, diabetes complications, respiratory infections, and adverse pregnancy outcomes. Oral health care is preventive medicine.

Tooth Decay

Caused by bacteria producing acids that erode enamel. Frequent sugar consumption with poor brushing increases risk rapidly.

Preventable with daily brushing

Gum Disease

Begins as gingivitis — red, swollen, bleeding gums — and can progress to periodontitis, destroying the bone supporting your teeth.

Linked to heart disease & diabetes

Oral Cancer

Affects lips, tongue, cheeks, and throat. See a professional for any sore or patch that doesn't heal within two weeks.

Early detection is critical

Daily Oral Hygiene

  1. 1
    Brush 2 minutes, twice daily
    Fluoride toothpaste, soft-bristle brush, 45° angle to gums, gentle circular motions. Brush your tongue too.
  2. 2
    Floss daily
    Remove plaque from between teeth where brushes can't reach. Prevents gum disease and inter-proximal cavities.
  3. 3
    Limit sugar, drink water
    Reduce sugary drinks and snacks. Drink fluoridated water. Eat calcium-rich foods — milk, yogurt, leafy greens.
  4. 4
    Visit a dentist every 6 months
    Routine check-ups for prevention and early detection. Even with no pain, regular visits are essential for everyone.

Oral-Systemic Connections

Diabetes

Gum disease makes blood sugar harder to control. Treating gum disease can improve glucose management.

Heart Disease

Bacteria from infected gums enter the bloodstream and contribute to arterial inflammation, increasing heart attack and stroke risk.

Pregnancy

Gum disease during pregnancy is associated with premature birth and low birth weight. Dental care is essential for expectant mothers.

Respiratory Conditions

Oral bacteria can be inhaled into the lungs, worsening pneumonia and other respiratory infections.
